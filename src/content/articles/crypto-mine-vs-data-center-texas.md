You see a big metal building going up on the rural lot down the road. Maybe rows of shipping-container-sized boxes. Maybe a low warehouse humming behind a new fence. Is it a crypto mine or a data center? To a worried neighbor, the question matters, because the two facilities can affect your land, your sleep, and your property value in different ways.

Here in Texas, both are spreading fast. Knowing which one you're dealing with helps you understand what to expect and what concerns may be worth raising.

## The core difference: what each one is for

The simplest way to tell them apart is purpose.

A **data center** is a building full of servers that store and process information. Think websites, business software, video streaming, and increasingly, artificial intelligence. The work is varied, and many data centers run a mix of equipment.

A **crypto mine** has one job: running specialized computers around the clock to solve the math puzzles that earn cryptocurrency. These machines, often called ASICs, are built to do that single task as fast as possible, and they rarely pause.

That difference in purpose drives almost everything a neighbor notices.

### Different hardware, different behavior

- **Data centers** often vary their power use based on demand. They tend to invest heavily in design, sometimes including quieter cooling and more polished construction.
- **Crypto mines** typically run flat-out, 24/7, to maximize earnings. The setups can be barer-bones: rows of machines in metal containers or open-sided structures, cooled by huge banks of industrial fans.

## Why crypto mines are notorious for noise

If neighbors are losing sleep, the facility is often a crypto mine.

The reason is cooling. Those mining machines generate intense heat and must be cooled constantly. Many operations rely on large arrays of high-speed fans that produce a relentless, droning roar. Because the machines run nonstop, the noise rarely lets up, day or night.

Some residents living near these operations have described a sound like a jet engine that never lands, or a constant low hum that carries for long distances across open Texas land. Sound travels far in quiet rural areas, so even a facility set back from the road can reach homes well beyond the fence line.

Data centers make noise too, often from cooling systems and backup generators. But many use water-based or enclosed cooling that can be quieter than wall-to-wall fans, and design choices may soften the impact. The key word is *may* — a poorly sited data center can still be a serious nuisance.

When noise becomes constant and interferes with daily life, it can raise questions under Texas **private nuisance** principles and the right to **quiet enjoyment** of your property. ## Water and the grid: shared strains

Noise gets the headlines, but both facility types can pressure two resources Texans care deeply about: water and electricity.

**Water.** Many large computing facilities use water for cooling, sometimes through evaporative cooling systems that lose water to the air as it does its job. In a state that knows drought well, heavy water use by a single facility can become a real concern for neighbors who depend on the same wells or local supply. Our [water harm page](/damage/water) goes deeper on this issue.

**The grid.** Both crypto mines and large data centers draw enormous amounts of electricity. That demand falls on ERCOT, the operator of the Texas power grid. Crypto mines in particular have drawn attention for their around-the-clock appetite, though large AI data centers are now competing for the same power. Heavy local demand can raise questions about reliability and cost for everyone connected nearby.

## Nuisance and property concerns: how they compare

For a landowner next door, here is a rough sense of what each tends to raise:

**Crypto mine — most common concerns:**
- Constant, loud fan noise that may disrupt sleep and daily life
- Visible industrial setups that can clash with rural character
- Water and power strain
- Possible effects on enjoyment and value of nearby property

**Data center — most common concerns:**
- Noise from cooling and generator testing, though sometimes less constant
- Large building footprint, lighting, and traffic during construction
- Significant water and power use
- Possible effects on the character and value of surrounding land

Both can affect your **quiet enjoyment** and, in some cases, your property's value. When a facility's operation substantially interferes with how you use and enjoy your land, Texas nuisance law may offer possible avenues to address it. ## What a neighbor can do

The first step is simply understanding what you're living next to and documenting what you experience: when the noise is worst, how it affects your household, and any changes you notice on your own land. Keeping a record could prove valuable later.
